The Way of Water

Half-Life 2 Single-Player FPS Level

A roughly ten-minute FPS level built around a predictable water-and-electricity rule, combining environmental hazards, timed traversal, combat, and a rising-water escape.

Core Mechanic#

The level takes place inside a damaged underwater facility. Water is not decorative; it is the rule that connects the entire experience. Electrified water first blocks progress, shutting down the power creates a limited traversal window, and the final rise in water level turns a previously controllable rule into sustained escape pressure.

Design Goal

Use one environmental rule for teaching, variation, and climax rather than introducing a series of unrelated mechanics.

Ten-Minute Experience Structure#

01

Establish Context

The player enters a damaged underwater base and reads the loss of control through environmental conditions.

02

Demonstrate Danger

Electricity, sparks, and blocked routes teach the relationship between water and the power system before the player is exposed to high risk.

03

Create a Window

The player shuts down power and crosses an otherwise inaccessible flooded area within a limited time.

04

Increase Pressure

Water begins to rise, turning a controllable rule into a threat that continuously advances.

05

Recombine Learning

Oxygen, flashlight use, vertical movement, and weapons are brought together during the final escape.

Player Guidance & Combat Pacing#

Hazard placement, doors, lighting, sound cues, and visible destinations direct attention. Important information is communicated through space and environmental response first, with explicit prompts used only when necessary. Combat alternates between compact rooms, wider areas, and vertical segments to avoid repeating the same shooting rhythm.

An underwater crossbow, oxygen management, and flashlight limitations add pressure to movement and combat. Bells and environmental changes communicate the timed window without requiring the player to monitor UI constantly.

Iteration Focus#

Playtest feedback led me to revise objective communication, access methods, enemy combinations, cover density, and route cues. Some interactions that originally depended on keys or isolated text prompts were replaced with clearer passwords, environmental clues, and cookie trails. Combat spaces gained stronger tactical identity through additional obstacles, enemy variety, and controlled sightlines.

Takeaways#

This project helped me understand the relationship between visible distance and effective combat distance in FPS levels. Levels do not simply maximize long sightlines; corners, occlusion, entrance buffers, and cover must control engagement range while preserving enough visual information to communicate destination and orientation.
