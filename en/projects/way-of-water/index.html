The Way of Water

Half-Life 2 Single-Player FPS Level

A ten-minute single-player level built around a predictable water-and-electricity rule set, combining environmental hazards, timed traversal, combat, and a rising-water escape.

Core Mechanic#

The level takes place in a damaged underwater facility. Water is not set dressing; it is the rule carrier for the entire experience. When electrified, it becomes an environmental hazard. After power is disabled, it creates a temporary traversal window. In the climax, rising water transforms the previously learned rule into continuous escape pressure.

Design Goal

Use one coherent environmental rule for onboarding, variation, and climax rather than introducing a sequence of unrelated mechanics.

Ten-Minute Experience Structure#

01

Establish the Situation

The player enters a damaged underwater base and reads its unstable state through the environment.

02

Demonstrate the Hazard

Electricity, sparks, and blocked routes establish the relationship between water and the power system.

03

Create a Window

The player shuts down power and crosses previously impassable flooded space within a limited time.

04

Raise the Pressure

Water begins to rise, turning a controllable rule into a persistent pursuit threat.

05

Recombine the Mechanic

Oxygen, flashlight use, vertical movement, and weapon handling converge in the final escape.

Player Guidance & Combat Pacing#

Hazard placement, doors, lighting, audio cues, and visible goals manage player attention. Critical information is communicated first through space and environmental feedback, with explicit prompts used only when necessary. Combat alternates between tight interiors, more open areas, and vertical sequences to avoid sustaining one shooting rhythm for too long.

The underwater crossbow, oxygen management, and flashlight resources add pressure to both movement and combat. Bells and environmental changes function as countdown feedback, letting players read timing without constantly checking UI.

Iteration Priorities#

Playtest feedback led me to revise objective communication, access methods, enemy composition, cover density, and route cues. Several objectives that originally relied on keys or isolated text were replaced with clearer passwords, environmental clues, and cookie trails. Combat spaces gained stronger tactical differences through new obstacles, enemy types, and sightline control.

Key Takeaway#

This project helped me understand the relationship between visible distance and effective firing distance in FPS level design. A level should not simply maximize long sightlines; corners, occlusion, entry buffers, and cover need to regulate engagement range while still preserving visual information that points players toward their goal.
