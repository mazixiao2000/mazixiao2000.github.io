From a Broad Concept to a Clear Core#

The project initially asked players to use a fishing rod for platforming, combat, and fishing, but pursuing several directions at once left the experience unfocused. After a scope review, the team narrowed the project into a precision platformer and concentrated on whether movement itself could carry the game.

Early testing still showed that the prototype lacked a strong decision-making tension and the project was close to being cut. I proposed adding a time-manipulation mechanic that lets players shift between fast execution and deliberate judgment. This change improved the feel of the controls and created a durable design variable for later levels.

Teaching the Mechanics & Building Difficulty#

I designed most of the game’s levels, including Levels 1, 2, 3, 5, 12, and 16. Early stages isolate movement, grappling, and time control so players can understand each action in a low-risk setting. Later stages combine those skills through tighter landing windows, chained actions, spatial compression, and higher failure costs.

01

One Variable at a Time

Each teaching segment first emphasizes a single action or judgment to avoid stacking pressure during initial learning.

02

Safe Practice

Low-cost spaces let players repeat an action and connect input with outcome.

03

Combined Validation

Previously learned actions are recombined into longer movement sequences that require planning.

04

Pacing Variation

Short recovery beats between demanding sequences prevent fatigue from obscuring learning feedback.

Playtesting & Collaboration#

During public playtests, we tracked failure locations, misunderstood inputs, and repeated attempts, then adjusted platform spacing, hazard boundaries, teaching order, and visual cues. Working in a small team required fast decisions between design ambition and implementation cost while keeping mechanics, levels, and feedback aligned.

Key Takeaway#

FLING taught me that the value of a core mechanic is not only its novelty, but whether it can generate enough clear and varied level-design problems. Time manipulation worked because it affected movement rhythm, decision time, route planning, and psychological pressure simultaneously—giving the game a strong basis for sustained difficulty progression.
