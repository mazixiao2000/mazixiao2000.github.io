## Project & Responsibilities

*Hamsterballin’* is a local multiplayer racing game where players roll and bounce hamster balls through tracks filled with hazards, shortcuts, and large elevation changes. I was responsible for the theme, route layout, pacing, and implementation collaboration of the final track, “Gacha Galaxy,” and proposed the cableway system during the POCT phase.

:::callout title="Design Goal"
In a fast, multiplayer, visually dense racing environment, players should identify the main route and optional shortcuts quickly. Camera movement and elevated travel should preview upcoming track structure and reduce disorientation.
:::

## Final Track: Gacha Galaxy

The track uses giant capsule machines, mechanical facilities, and layered rails as its visual and spatial identity. Continuous elevation shifts, bounce sequences, width changes, and rejoin points shape the sensation of speed. The main route stays stable and readable, while shortcuts create choices through risk, execution demand, and visual attraction.

I worked continuously with artists, programmers, and other designers to align collision, boundaries, asset placement, and camera requirements so route readability, racing rhythm, and final visual quality remained coherent.

## Cableways as Both Shortcuts and Navigation Tools

The cableway began as an optional shortcut, but iteration expanded its role. Once players leave the ground route, the camera can frame landmarks, preview the next section, reinforce global orientation, and create a brief pacing change within otherwise high-speed driving.

:::steps
- Readable Entry | Track direction, color, and elevation make each cableway entrance visible before players reach it at speed.
- Travel Presentation | The camera shifts from driving follow to a more stable composition that reveals track structure and destination.
- Route Preview | Camera motion deliberately passes over the next road segment, turn, or landmark to prepare players for re-entry.
- Smooth Return | At the exit, camera and control transition back to the player without disrupting racing judgment.
:::

## Team Collaboration & Iteration

In a 42-person team, level design cannot progress independently of production constraints. I translated design intent into actionable needs, confirmed interaction and camera logic with programmers, aligned landmarks, materials, and readability with artists, and used multiplayer playtests to check whether players noticed entrances, understood routes, and rejoined the track successfully.

The project taught me how to preserve a design goal within a large Scrum team—not by defending an initial solution, but by continuously negotiating performance, production volume, visual quality, and gameplay clarity.
