## Project & Responsibilities

*Hamsterballin’* is a local multiplayer racing game in which players control rolling and bouncing hamster balls across tracks filled with hazards, shortcuts, and large elevation changes. I led theme development, route layout, pacing, and implementation coordination for the final level, “Gacha Galaxy,” and proposed and developed the cableway system beginning in the POCT phase.

:::callout title="Design Goal"
In a fast, visually dense multiplayer race, players should be able to identify the main route and optional shortcuts quickly. Camera movement and elevated traversal should also preview upcoming track structure and reduce disorientation.
:::

## Final Track: Gacha Galaxy

The track is built around giant capsule machines, mechanical structures, and multilayered rails. Continuous elevation shifts, bounce sequences, changes in route width, and merge points create a strong sense of speed. The main route stays readable and reliable, while shortcuts create choices through risk, execution difficulty, and visual attraction.

I worked continuously with artists, engineers, and other designers to align collision, track boundaries, asset placement, and camera requirements so gameplay readability, race pacing, and final visual quality remained consistent.

## Cableway System: Turning a Shortcut into a Navigation Tool

The cableway began as an optional shortcut, but iteration expanded its role. While the player leaves the ground track, a controlled camera can present landmarks, preview the next segment, reinforce global orientation, and provide a brief pacing change between high-intensity driving sections.

:::steps
- Entry Readability | Track direction, color, and elevation allow players to see the cableway entrance before reaching it at speed.
- Camera Presentation | The camera shifts from reactive driving follow to a more stable composition that presents the environment and route structure.
- Route Preview | Camera movement deliberately reveals the next road, turn, merge point, or major landmark before control resumes.
- Smooth Return | Control and the driving camera are restored gradually so the transition does not interfere with race judgment.
:::

## Iteration & Team Production

Multiplayer playtests exposed problems that single-player testing could not: congestion at entrances, unclear route ownership, camera competition, and uneven shortcut value. I used those observations to adjust entrance width, camera timing, route visibility, and rejoin positioning while coordinating with multiple disciplines through Scrum milestones.
